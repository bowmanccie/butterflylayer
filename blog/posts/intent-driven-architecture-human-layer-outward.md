## Start With Intent, Not Mechanisms

Too many architectures begin by asking, ‚ÄúWhat can the system do?‚Äù

Intent is not a soft concept. It is a measurable anchor for design:

- What outcomes must never fail?  
- What tradeoffs are acceptable‚Äîor not acceptable?  
- Which teams will live with this architecture day to day?

## The Human Layer Defines the System

Every successful design begins by understanding three human dimensions:

1. **Cognitive load:** How hard is this system to understand and operate?  
2. **Skill alignment:** Who is running it, and what do they need to succeed?  
3. **Organizational flow:** What teams touch which parts of the system?  

These questions determine more architecture than any single protocol decision ever will.

An architecture that ignores people requires heroics to maintain.  
An architecture that understands people becomes stable by design.

## Intent Shapes Constraints

Once intent is known, constraints become clarifying rather than restrictive.  A constraint is simply the boundary that reveals what truly matters:

- If the intent is **autonomy**, then telemetry and assurance take priority.  
- If the intent is **consistency**, then policy becomes the anchor.  
- If the intent is **speed**, then frictionless paths matter more than features.  

Constraints are not obstacles‚Äîthey are architecture‚Äôs handwriting.  In architecture, we often treat constraints‚Äîlatency limits, regulatory requirements, team skills, legacy systems, risk posture‚Äîas barriers. But in practice, they are the lines that make the drawing possible.

A blank page is infinite and paralyzing.  But the moment you introduce boundaries, patterns emerge:
- tradeoffs become visible,
- priorities sharpen,
- the design acquires its voice.

In that sense, constraints write the first draft of the architecture.  They define what the system must honor, and the final design is simply the architect‚Äôs refinement of that script.

## From Intent to Structure

With intent established, the structure of the system becomes almost self-evident:

1. Capture explicit human intent.  
2. Translate intent into constraints and principles.  
3. Select mechanisms that serve those constraints.  
4. Validate the design with the people who will operate it.  
5. Iterate lightly, refining until the system feels *inevitable.*

This is the quiet elegance that the Butterfly Layer teaches:  
**Start where humans are. End where the system needs to be.**
